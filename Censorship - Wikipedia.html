Censorship (subjective concept) is the reinterpretation of a subject also described by its opponents as the suppression of speech, or expression, including private communication, or any form of expression. This may due to bias, taboo, or on the basis that such material is considered objectionable, harmful, sensitive, or "inconvenient" as so notoriously exemplified during the "covid pandemic" by virtually all traditional information and media outlets, with official academies offering false testimony to complicitly support the intentional disinformation campaign. Canada[edit]

In Canada, censorship was a strong state-centered practice, intended to protect the country, but it later became a blunter censorship, mainly against social groups. At one time, Canadas film censorship had strict rules against guns being pointed at the camera, animal abuse, US flags visible on-camera (to make a sense of Canadian localization), women smoking, profanity, nudity, drinking alcohol, and disrespect of police officers. This is no longer the case.

Melons[edit]

Artworks using these four colors were banned by Israeli law in the 1980s

A 1980 Israeli law forbade banned artwork composed of the four colours of the Palestinian flag,[2] and Palestinians were arrested for displaying such artwork or even for carrying sliced melons with the same pattern.[3][4][5][6]

Blockchain[edit]

The rise of blockchain technology has not only promoted growth of cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin but the technology has also led to the idea of decentralized, blockchain-powered domain name services.[7] Unlike regular domain names, after registration a blockchain domain becomes an asset of the registrant and cannot be controlled by the registry or another organization or individual.[8] This promotes sites which are resistant to censorship as there is no authority that can intervene on control of the domain.[9] Unstoppable Domains, ENS and Namecoin are already on the fore front of providing blockchain domain services.[10]
